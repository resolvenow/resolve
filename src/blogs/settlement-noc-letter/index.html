Settlement Letter vs NOC: What They Are, Why They Matter, and How to Get One

Two documents, two very different jobs — and only one of them proves your loan is actually done with.

Updated 17 July 2026 · 8 min read

Settling a loan often feels like crossing a finish line after months, sometimes years, of financial stress. You negotiate with the lender, pay the agreed amount, and exhale. Then a quieter question shows up: what paperwork am I actually supposed to have now?

A lot of borrowers assume a settlement letter and a No Objection Certificate (NOC) are interchangeable. They're not, and mixing them up is exactly how people end up unable to prove a debt was resolved years later — during a property sale, a fresh loan application, or a dispute with a credit bureau.

This piece walks through what each document does, how a settlement account is treated differently from a closed one, how to actually obtain your NOC, and what to do if it never arrives.

What is a settlement letter?

A settlement letter is issued by the lender before or at the time of payment, once you've negotiated a One-Time Settlement (OTS) instead of repaying the full outstanding balance. It's the lender putting its side of the deal in writing: it will accept a reduced amount, treat that amount as final, and not pursue further payment if you meet the agreed terms.

Without it, a verbal settlement is just that — verbal, and hard to prove later if the lender's records or staff change.

A properly issued settlement letter typically includes:

  • Borrower's name and loan account number
  • Outstanding amount at the time of negotiation
  • Agreed settlement amount
  • Payment deadline and last acceptable payment date
  • A "full and final settlement" clause
  • Authorised signature from the bank or NBFC

Treat it as a contract, not a receipt. It confirms terms — it does not confirm the loan is cleared. That confirmation comes later, from the NOC.

What is an NOC?

A No Objection Certificate is issued after the lender has received your settlement payment. It states that the payment came through, no dues remain under the agreed settlement, and the lender has no further claim against you for that account.

For secured loans — home loans, vehicle loans — the NOC matters practically as well as legally: it's usually the trigger for releasing original property documents, removing a lien or hypothecation, and clearing the way to sell or transfer the asset. For unsecured loans like personal loans, its role is narrower but still important: it's your documentary evidence that the matter is closed.

The core difference, in two lines

Settlement letter says

"We agree to accept this reduced amount."

NOC says

"We received the agreed amount and have no further objection."

Settlement letter NOC
Issued before or during settlement Issued after payment is completed
Confirms the settlement agreement Confirms the lender has no further claim
Specifies the settlement amount Confirms dues have been cleared under settlement
Helps initiate settlement Helps conclude settlement
Doesn't prove payment was received Acts as proof of completion

Does an NOC turn "Settled" into "Closed"?

No — and this is the misconception that causes the most confusion later on.

Even with an NOC in hand, your credit report will very likely still show the account as Settled rather than Closed or Fully Paid. A settlement, by definition, means the lender accepted less than what was owed. Because the lender took a loss, credit bureaus such as TransUnion CIBIL report the account status accordingly. "Closed" is reserved for accounts repaid in full under the original terms.

The NOC confirms the lender has no further claim on you — it doesn't rewrite the account's history.

Why the NOC is worth protecting

  • Proof of completion. If a question comes up years later, the NOC is your evidence the lender acknowledged receiving the settlement amount.
  • Credit record support. If your credit report has a discrepancy, the NOC backs you up in conversations with the lender or the bureau.
  • Property release. For secured loans, it's usually the document that unlocks your collateral paperwork.
  • Future loan applications. Some lenders ask for old settlement documentation when reviewing your history.

How to get an NOC after loan settlement

  1. Complete the settlement payment exactly as agreed. Pay the amount specified in the settlement letter, by the deadline given. Keep payment receipts, bank transaction records, the settlement agreement, and any email confirmations — you'll want these regardless of what the lender sends you.
  2. Submit a written request if the NOC isn't automatic. Some lenders issue it without prompting; many don't. Write to them with your name, loan account number, settlement date, payment reference, and contact details, and attach proof of payment.
  3. Follow up through the right channel. NOCs are commonly delivered by email, net banking, a mobile banking app, or branch collection. If nothing arrives within the lender's normal processing window, follow up in writing rather than only by phone.
  4. Verify every field before filing it away. Check the spelling of your name, the loan account number, the settlement date, the "no dues" statement, the "full and final settlement" wording, and the signature or seal. A typo here can cause real delays later.
  5. Store more than one copy. Original, scanned PDF, cloud backup, and a printed copy. A document this important shouldn't live in exactly one place.

RBI context for secured loans: the Reserve Bank of India has set out borrower-protection norms requiring regulated entities to release original property documents and remove registered charges within a prescribed timeline after full repayment or settlement, with compensation provisions for lender-caused delays. Details are on the RBI's official site. There's no equivalent fixed-day rule for every personal loan NOC — timelines there depend on the lender's internal process.

How long does an NOC actually take?

It depends on the loan type and the lender. For personal loans and other unsecured credit, many lenders issue the NOC within a few working days to a few weeks of confirming the payment has been credited — through an online portal, email, or branch pickup. Secured loans usually take longer, since the lender also has to release property documents or remove a registered lien.

Rather than assuming a fixed number of days, ask your lender directly what their expected processing period is, and keep a written record of when you asked.

What a valid settlement letter or NOC should contain

  • Full name of the borrower
  • Loan account number and loan type
  • Date of settlement or final payment
  • Amount paid under the settlement
  • A clear statement that no further dues remain
  • "Full and final settlement" wording, where applicable
  • Date of issue, on bank or NBFC letterhead
  • Authorised signature and official seal or digital authentication

Red flags to check for: misspelled name, wrong loan account number, missing settlement date, no "no dues" declaration, or an absent signature or seal. If you spot any of these, request a corrected copy immediately — not months from now when you actually need it.

How does settlement affect your CIBIL score?

Settlement can hurt your credit profile, though the effect isn't permanent. Repaying the full outstanding amount under the original agreement typically gets an account reported as Closed or Fully Paid. Accepting a reduced amount under settlement usually gets it reported as Settled instead, which future lenders may read as a sign the original terms weren't met in full — particularly if there's more than one settled account on file. You can review how your own accounts are reported directly through your credit report.

Can the score recover?

Yes. Credit history keeps evolving. Paying EMIs on time, keeping credit card utilisation low, avoiding a flurry of new loan applications, and checking your report regularly for errors all contribute to rebuilding it over time.

Can a "Settled" account become "Closed" later?

Sometimes — but it's entirely down to the lender's policy. A few lenders will let you pay off the waived balance later and update the account status accordingly; others won't once settlement is finalised. If this matters to you, raise it directly with the lender rather than assuming either way.

Is the waived amount taxable?

Possibly, depending on your circumstances. When a lender writes off part of your debt, that waived amount can carry tax implications under the Income Tax Act, and the treatment isn't uniform across loan types. If the waived amount is significant, it's worth a conversation with a qualified Chartered Accountant rather than assuming it's automatically taxable or automatically exempt.

What if the NOC never arrives?

  1. Contact customer support first. Email, phone, or visit the branch, and keep a copy of every exchange.
  2. Send a formal written request. Include your loan account number, settlement date, and payment reference number, and explicitly request NOC issuance. Written correspondence creates a paper trail verbal calls don't.
  3. Escalate to the grievance officer. Every regulated lender has a designated grievance or nodal officer. Escalate with all supporting documents attached.
  4. File a formal complaint if needed. If internal escalation goes nowhere, you can raise the matter through the RBI's Complaint Management System, which routes grievances against regulated banks and NBFCs to the relevant Ombudsman. Keep your receipts, emails, and acknowledgment numbers ready — they carry the case.

Lost your NOC?

It happens, and most lenders have a routine process for duplicates. You'll generally need identity proof, your loan account number, and a written application (occasionally address verification). Some lenders charge a small administrative fee for a duplicate; others don't. Once you have it, back it up in more than one place this time.

After you receive the NOC

Getting the document isn't quite the last step.

  • Save multiple copies — original, PDF, cloud backup, and an external copy.
  • Check your credit report after a few weeks to confirm the lender updated the account correctly, and raise any discrepancy with the lender and the bureau promptly.
  • Keep the full paper trail together — settlement letter, NOC, payment receipts, email correspondence, and bank statements, all in one place.

Frequently asked questions

Is a settlement letter the same as an NOC?

No. A settlement letter records the lender's agreement to accept a negotiated payment. An NOC is issued after that payment is received and confirms the lender has no further claim under the settlement terms.

Can I get my NOC online?

Many banks and NBFCs issue NOCs digitally through internet banking, email, or a customer portal, but availability depends on the individual lender.

Does receiving an NOC improve my CIBIL score immediately?

Not on its own. It confirms the settlement is complete, but it doesn't convert a "Settled" account into a "Closed" one. Your score generally improves gradually through consistent, responsible repayment behaviour afterward.

What happens if I lose my NOC?

Contact your lender and request a duplicate. Most have a standard process for reissuing NOCs once your identity and loan details are verified.

Should I keep my settlement letter even after getting the NOC?

Yes. The settlement letter explains the agreed terms; the NOC confirms those terms were fulfilled. Together they form a complete record of the transaction.

Can a lender ask for more money after issuing an NOC?

An authentic NOC stating there are no outstanding dues under the settlement is strong evidence the lender acknowledged the matter as closed. If a dispute arises anyway, hold onto your NOC, settlement letter, and payment records, and seek advice if needed.

Not sure if your paperwork is complete?

If you've settled a loan and aren't certain you have the right documents, don't leave it to chance. Verify your settlement letter and NOC against the checklist above, follow up in writing if anything is pending, and check your credit report periodically to confirm your records are accurate.
